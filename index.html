Origin

The earliest known history of the Newar and Kathmandu Valley is mixed with myths recorded in historical chronicles. One such text that tells the formation of the valley is Swayambpurana. According to this Buddhist scripture, the Valley of Kathmandu was a vast lake until Bodhisattva Manjushri, with the help of the Holy Sword, cut through the gaps in the surrounding hills and drained water. Backed by geological evidence of the ancient lake bottom, this apocalyptic legend explains the high fertility of the soil in the Kathmandu Valley.

According to Swayamb Prana, the Manjushri later established a city called the Manjushri. Sanskrit for "land founded by the Manjushri”, now called the Manjushri, and Dharmakara. I made it a king. Magiper has another shrine dedicated to Manjushri. From this era until the arrival of the Gopal era, no historical documents were found. The genealogy of the king is recorded in a chronicle called Gopalara javamsavali. According to this manuscript, King Gopal was taken over by Mahispar and Kirat before the Licchavi royal invasion from the south. Some claim that the Buddha visited Nepal during the reign of King Kirat Zitedusti. The Richavi dynasty ruled for at least 600 years, followed by the Malla dynasty in the 12th century.

Language

Although "Nepal Basa" is classified as Sino-Tibetan, many of its grammars, words and vocabularies are derived from the influence of South Indian languages ​​such as Sanskrit, Prakrit and Maithili. The Newar are connected by a common language and culture.Their common language is Nepal Bhasa or the linguistic progenitor of that language. Nepal Bhasa is the term recognized by the government.

Newar Basa is a language that was spoken as early as the Richavi era and is believed to have evolved from the language spoken in Nepal during the Kirat era. The Nepal Basa inscription appeared in the 12th century, the first example being a manuscript of Ukubaha palm leaves. Nepalese Basa developed as a court and official language in the 14th and late 18th centuries. It has been universally used in stone and bronze inscriptions, sacred manuscripts, official documents, diaries, diplomas, correspondence, and creative writing.

Clothing

Western wear is the norm in urban areas in the rest of the country. Traditionally men wearntapuli (cap), a long shirt (tapālan), and trousers (suruwā), also called DauraSuruwal. Women wear cheeparsi (sari) and gaa (long length shawl) while younger girls wear anklelength gowns (bhāntānlan). Ritual dresses consist of pleated gowns, coats, and a variety of headresses. Jyapu women have a distinctive sari called Hāku Patāsi which is a black sari with a distinctive red border. Jap Men also have a unique version of Tapar Lansulwa. Similarly, shawls (gā) are worn by both men and women. Traditionally, Newar women wear red cloth shoes, Kaparakan. She is adorned with glitter and colored beads (potia). One of the main parts of Newar clothing is the bracelet (Chula) and Mara (necklace).
